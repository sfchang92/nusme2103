8.1 Keys

A key is a detail inserted axially between a shaft and a hub to prevent relative rotation of the parts.

Keys are made from steel to resist adequately the high shearing and crushing stresses place on them.

8.1.1 Classes of fit for keys

Three classes of fit are provided for the keys:

Free where the hub is required to slide over the key when in use
Normal where the key is to be inserted in the keyway with the minimum fitting, as in mass production assembly
Close where an accurate fit of key is required

8.1.2 Key types

8.1.2.1 Square & rectangular keys

Chamfers are necessary in keys as sharp corners in the keyway would act as stress raiser with a consequent risk of fracture at these points.

Taper keys are used to transmit heavy unidirectional, reversing and vibrating torque, and where periodic withdrawal of the key may be necessary. The Gib head makes withdrawal of the key possible.

8.1.2.2 Woodruff keys

A Woodruff key is in a form of a segment of a circle and fits in a corresponding recess in the shaft.

It adjusts itself to any taper or the hub keyway and is widely used on the machine tool.

It has the disadvantage that the deep keyway weakens the shaft.

8.1.2.3 Round keys

8.1.2.4 Saddle keys

Saddle keys are suitable only for light torques.

8.1.3 Splines

Splines are projections of rectangular cross-section machined on a shaft. They are equally spaced round the shaft and vary in number from 4 onwards.

If one position assembly is required, one spline may be left uncut on the shaft and one removed from the hub.

The shaft and hub will then assemble in one position only.

8.1.4 Serrations

Serrations are similar to splines but are triangular in cross-section instead of rectangular.

Since they are smaller than splines the angular relationship between shaft and hub may be closely controlled.

8.2 Couplings

Couplings are used to connect shafts so that rotation may be transmitted from one shaft to another.

8.2.1 Flange coupling

Flange coupling consists of a flange mounted on the end of one shaft and bolted to a companion flange on the other shaft.

A projection of one flange is allowed to recess into the other in order to provide positive shaft alignment.

8.2.2 Hookes coupling / Universal joint

Hookes coupling is used to connect two non-parallel shafts.

Uniform angular velocity of the input shaft produces non-uniform angular velocity of the output shaft. For this reason two universal joints are always used in an installation.

8.2.3 Flexible coupling

Flexible couplings are used to connect shafts that may deviate slightly in alignment.

The several forms available consist in general of two rigid hub-mounted flanges connected with a flexible member.

8.2.4 Oldham coupling

Oldham coupling is used to transmit power between two shafts that are parallel but not aligned.

With this form of coupling, the angular velocities of the coupled shafts are identical.

8.2.5 Clamp coupling

A clamp coupling is made in two halves, bolted together with strips of spacing material between the halves prior to boring the coupling to the shaft size.

8.3 Locking devices

Locking devices are of two main types:

  • Frictional – e.g. Lock Nut, Lock Washer
  • Positive – e.g. Split Pin, Taper Pin

8.3.1 Lock nut

A thin nut is first tightened down and a normal nut is screwed down on top of it.

The thin nut is then slackened back slightly, thus wedges the nut threads against opposite flanks of the bolt threads and jams the nuts in place.

Below the screw, the hatching crosses the internal thread.

8.3.2 Split pin

The end of the bolt is turned down to a plain diameter for a short distance and a hole drilled through it. A split pin is driven through the hole and the legs bent over the end of the bolt and the other over a flat on the nut.

8.3.3 Taper pin

Taper pin is a very safe positive locking device particularly if the small end of the taper pin is split and the legs are opened out.

A disadvantage is that if the nut is removed, it is very difficult to line up the holes in the nut and bolt again. The hole also weakens both details.

8.3.4 Lock washers

When a nut is tightened down on a spring washer, the washer thrusts the nut threads against the bolt threads, thus increasing the friction between them.

8.3.5 Stiff nut

The cylindrical rim is slotted to form two arms which are deflected down slightly to de-pitch the thread in them.

When the nut is fitted to a bolt, the arms are forced up, thus producing friction between the nut and bolt threads

3.1 Sectioning

3.1.1 Types of sectioning

3.1.1.1 Full section

The cutting planes passes completely through the object.

All visible edges behind the planes must be shown or the view will be incomplete.

Example - Bearing bracket

3.1.1.2 Half section

Enable the shape description of the outside and inside of a component to be whoen in the minimum number of view.

Only symmetrical components are drawn this way.

Hidden lines are shown on the outside view only.

The halves of a half section should be separated by a center line and not an outline.

3.1.1.3 Offset section

The cutting plane need not pass straight through the object, but may be offset as required to include features which are not in a straight line.

3.1.1.4 Aligned section

Section by rotating the cutting plane to a position which will pass through more than one features and then treating it as a single plane.

Example - Indicator sleeve

3.1.1.5 Broken-out or part section

When a part of a view in seciton is all that is needed to show the internal details of an object.

Sometimes a whole view cannot be sectioned because it would conceal external features which must be shown.

3.1.1.6 Revolved section

Section drawn directly on an outside view to show the local cross-section of an arm or rib.

The cutting plane is revolved through 90 °.

3.1.1.7 Removed section

Sections are removed to another part of the drawing instead of beaing drawn on the outside view.

3.1.2 Exceptions to sectioning

Reinforcing ribs or webs of a casting are not cross hatched when the cutting plane passes through them longitudinally.

Gear teeth are treated in a similar way.

Shaft, bolts, nuts, rods, rivets, keys, pins and similar parts are normally not sectioned.

3.1.5 Drawing hatch lines

Sectional view is distinguished from outside view by section lines or hatching drawn on the cut surfaces.

Thin lines (0.3mm), inclined at 45° are used.

Equally spaced by eye (4-6mm).

When the outlines or axes of a sectional view slope at 45°, then the sectional lines should be drawn at some other angle.

3.2 Dimensioning

3.2.1 Principles of dimensioning

  1. Each dimension necessary for the complete definition of a finished product should be given on the drawing and should appear once only.
  2. There should be no more dimensions than are necessary to define the component.
  3. Dimensions relating to a particular feature should preferably be place adjacent to a single suitable view rather than on several.

3.2.5 Chain dimensioning

Should only be used where the possible accumulation of tolerances does not endanger the functional requirements of the part.

3.2.6 Leader

  • Used to indicate where dimensions or notes are intend to apply.

  • Dimension repeated to avoid long leader.

  • Letter symbols to avoid long and intersecting leaders.

3.2.7 Linear dimensions

  • Drawings should state the limit of dimension.
  • Any feature which the standard designation is in imperial unit, then the imperial designation should be used. E.g a 5/8 in. bolt should be shown as 5/8 in.
  • Where drawings are dimensioned mainly in one unit abbreviation may be omitted provided the drawing carries a statement of the unit used.
  • Dimension should be expressed to the least number of significant figure. (E.g 35 not 35.0)
  • Decimal marker for use with metric units is a point which should be bold, should be given a full letter space and should be placed on the base line. (E.g 12.5)
  • Dimension less than unity should be preceded by a cipher 0. (E.g 0.5)
  • Strongly deprecated unless necessary, a conversion is shown in parentheses. E.g 100mm (3.94in.)

3.2.8 Angular dimensions

  • The radian is the preferred SI unit.
  • Angular dimensions on engineering drawings should be expressed in degrees, minutes and seconds.
    Example:
    • 22°
    • 22°30’
    • 22°30’30”

3.2.9 Arrangement of dimensions

  • Dimensions placed to read from the bottom or from the right hand side of the drawing.
  • Figures and letter should be large enough for easy reading.
  • Figures or letters should be placed near the middle of, and above and clear of the dimension.
  • To facilitate reading dimensions, figures should be placed so that they can be read from the bottom or from the right of the drawing.
  • When there are several parallel dimension lines the dimensions should be staggered.
  • Where narrow spaces are being dimensioned, figures or letters should be placed centrally or above the extension of one of the arrowheads.
  • Large dimensions should be placed outside smaller dimensions.

3.2.10 Redundant dimensions

Where an overall dimension is shown, one of the intermediate distances is redundant and should not be dimensioned.

3.2.11 Auxiliary dimensions

Auxiliary dimension should not be tolerated but should be included in parenthesis (....)
